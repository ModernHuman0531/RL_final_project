Towards Trustworthy Smart Cities: An Explainable and Safe DRL Application for Sustainable Traffic Management

Cheuk Hong Tang,  Jana Speldrich,  Dunkun Wei
Department of Computer Science, National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University
NYCU 535510 Reinforcement Learning — 2026
Code Poster

Abstract

Traffic signal control in urban environments must balance vehicle throughput with pedestrian safety — a requirement that classical rule-based methods cannot meet simultaneously. We present an Action-Constrained Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO) agent that learns adaptive signal timing while enforcing hard pedestrian safety constraints through feasible-action masking at every decision step. The constraint checker computes the feasible action set C(s) at each step, and the policy selects only from safe phases via masked softmax, so infeasible actions receive zero probability and can never be executed. The agent is evaluated against five baselines — Fixed-Time, Max Pressure, SOTL, DQN-AR, and SPRe+ — on a single-intersection toy task and a 3×3 grid network simulated in SUMO. The proposed PPO agent achieves the lowest waiting time among all safe agents and zero constraint violations in both scenarios, while all rule-based baselines violate safety constraints in 19–71% of steps.

Methodology

The agent operates within an Action-Constrained MDP. At every simulation step the constraint checker removes signal phases that would violate any hard safety rule (yellow-lock, minimum green 10 s, maximum green 60 s, pedestrian crossing occupied). PPO then selects actions via a masked softmax restricted to the remaining feasible set, so unsafe phases can never be executed — not by reward penalty, but by construction.

The state vector (31 dimensions) encodes: current phase index and phase timer, per-lane vehicle density and waiting time, and per-crossing pedestrian waiting time and occupancy. The reward is rt = −(α Wtv + β Wtp), penalising both vehicle and pedestrian waiting.

Baselines: Fixed-Time alternates NS/EW on a fixed 30 s cycle. Max Pressure (Varaiya 2013) selects the phase maximising queue pressure — vehicle-centric, no pedestrian safety. SOTL (Gershenson 2005) switches when red-side queue ≥ 5 and minimum green is satisfied. DQN-AR uses acceptance-rejection resampling for zero violations at O(1) cost. SPRe+ (Hung et al. 2025) projects the full policy onto the feasible simplex via QP — O(|A|²) per step.

Key Findings

  • PPO reduces average vehicle wait by 44% vs. Fixed-Time (1×1) and 20% vs. Fixed-Time (3×3).
  • The only agent with lower raw wait than PPO in 1×1 (Max Pressure, 55.6 s) violates safety in 53.5% of steps — unsafe for deployment.
  • All three learned agents achieve exactly 0% violations; all rule-based baselines violate in 19–71% of steps.
  • PPO's advantage shrinks in 3×3 (−6.4% vs. Max Pressure) vs. 1×1 (−16% vs. SOTL), consistent with the increased difficulty of a partially-controlled 9-intersection network.
